A defining morphologic feature of AML cells is the presence of enlarged nucleoli, shown here by the red arrows, which are sites of ribosomal RNA or rRNA synthesis. rRNA are specialized non-coding RNAs that form the catalytic cores of ribosomes. As you can see in this cryo-EM structure, rRNAs depicted as colored ribbons make up about half of the ribosomal complex. Their production is therefore an important control point in ribosome biogenesis, and more broadly, protein translation. However, the regulation of rRNA synthesis across different cell types and in cancer remains poorly defined. Within the nucleolus, ribosomal DNA is organized into hundreds of these nearly identical tandem repeats. These rDNA loci are transcribed by RNA polymerase I or Pol I, to initially generate a nascent 47S rRNA. This is a highly active process where you can have up to 100 Pol I molecules transcribing a single rDNA locus at a single time.The precursor of the nascent RNA is rapidly cleaved into these mature rRNAs that are then assembled into the 60S and 40S ribosomal subunits. Our big picture questions were then, one, how is rRNA synthesis differentially regulated during normal and malignant hematopoiesis? We obtained fresh bone marrow aspirates from healthy donors and AML patients at the hospital of the University of Pennsylvania, isolated the mononuclear cells, and performed antibody staining to define specific hematopoietic cell populations. We then fixed the cells and hybridized them with fluorescent probes targeting the rRNAs of interest, then measured the probe signal in each cell by flow cytometry.
