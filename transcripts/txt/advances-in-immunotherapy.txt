In conclusion, to our knowledge, we analyzed the largest cohort of IO resistance in non-small cell lung cancer, compiled with more than 900 patients from the HUDSON phase 2 clinical trial. We identified distinct genomic features associated with acquired resistance, along with genomic alterations in pathways such as cell cycle, DNA damage repair, and dedifferentiation. Whereas genomic instability was a shared feature between the two resistance groups. Acquired resistance was associated with upregulation of epithelial to mesenchymal translation transition, a downregulation of antigen presentation, an increased density of pluripotent stem-like cell states, and metabolic reprogramming, which together orchestrate the convergent evolution of IO resistance. The immune infiltrate at the time of acquired resistance showed dysfunctional interferon gamma signaling, along with an enrichment of hyperfunctional T cell states. Overall, our findings suggest that IO resistance is multifactorial, as cancer cells acquire a fitness advantage through differential activation of cancer hallmarks, cell states, and immune escape, pointing towards convergent evolution. The evolutionary cost of IO resistance may reveal actionable vulnerabilities that require personalized combination strategies in the post-IO setting to help overcome IO resistance.
