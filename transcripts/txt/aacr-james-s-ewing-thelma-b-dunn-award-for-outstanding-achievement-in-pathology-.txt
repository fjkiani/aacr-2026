Dr. Rim is an exceptional scientist and pathologist whose innovations in quantitative biomarker science have transformed cancer diagnosis and informed modern approaches to precision medicine. His work has pushed the boundaries of how we measure, interpret, and clinically apply biomarkers in human tissue. A central focus of Dr. Rim's career has been the development of rigorous quantitative methods for tissue biomarker research. In early studies of prognostic biomarkers in breast cancer, he identified a challenge, the lack of accurate and reproducible tools to quantify predictive biomarkers in tissue specimens. To address this, he invented the EquAsystem in 2002, a groundbreaking fluorescent-based platform for automated quantitative analysis. This innovation revolutionized immunohistochemistry, establishing a new benchmark for precision, and enabling the widespread adoption of quantitative pathology. Building on this work, Dr. Rim became an early leader in the use of tissue microarray, and his laboratory developed imaging pipelines for hotspot identification and quantitative analysis. These advances enabled essential studies into biomarker heterogeneity, including the clinical significance of EGFR and estrogen receptor variability in breast cancer. More recently, Dr. Rim has applied his biomarker expertise to cancer immunotherapy. His investigations into the prognostic and predictive role of PD-L1 expression across breast cancer, melanoma, and lung cancer demonstrated that PD-L1 alone is insufficient as a biomarker unless evaluated in the context of specific immune cell subsets and their spatial relationship to tumor cells. This insight has shaped modern approaches to immunotherapy biomarker assessment. Dr. Rim's contribution to biomarker science have had a profound impact. He has played a central role in national and international efforts to establish guidelines for biomarker evaluation, particularly for HER2, ER, and PR in breast cancer, directly improving diagnostic accuracy and patient care worldwide. Dr. Rim has been a dedicated AACR member since 2002, and his leadership, scholarship, and innovation have left an indelible mark in the field of pathology and on cancer research as a whole.
