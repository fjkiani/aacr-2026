Over the past two decades, the treatment of advanced non-small cell lung cancer has emerged as a success story of precision oncology. When a patient is diagnosed, we genotype their tumors to identify oncogenic mutations in driver genes that dictate which type of therapy the patient will receive. My lab has become interested in understanding the mechanisms that govern tumor evolution. But to describe the way we think about tumor evolution, I need to go back to this influential paper published in 2010 by Jeff Settleman and colleagues. They showed that when cancer cell lines were treated with targeted therapies, a small population of cancer cells could survive therapy. They termed these cells drug-tolerant persisters, borrowing terminology from bacterial persisters that had been described in the 1940s. Bacterial persisters are subpopulations of bacteria that can survive an environmental stress, such as an antibiotic, and then repopulate when the antibiotic or the environmental stress is removed. Importantly, bacterial persisters are distinct from resistant bacteria in that they do not harbor fixed genetic resistance. Similarly, drug-tolerant persisters do not harbor fixed resistance but are characterized by epigenetic plasticity creating drug tolerance that is reversible with withdrawal of the therapy. Subsequently, in our work, we observed that EGFR mutant cell lines consistently evolve T790M. However, they did so with varying frequencies. In some cases, resistance emerged very quickly after only a few weeks, while in other cases, it took months or years. We hypothesized that perhaps the late-evolving clones might represent evolution of resistance from drug-tolerant persisters. In subsequent work, we went on to demonstrate that yes, indeed, early resistant clones evolved from rare preexisting T790M clones that were present in the population, whereas late resistance evolved from persister cells that went on to de novo acquire resistance mutations such as EGFR T790M or other mechanisms of genomic resistance during the course of therapy. These results demonstrated that at least in preclinical models, clinical-acquired resistance could result from a multi-step evolutionary process in which persister cells subsequently evolve genomic mechanisms of resistance. But what was challenging was to know whether or not this happened in the clinic, owing to the limited nature of tumor sampling that we can achieve with clinical tumor biopsies. However, given the stress that these cells undergo as they're in the persister state, we hypothesized that perhaps there might be hallmarks or signatures of the stress that would be reflected in the resistant tumors that might tell us which evolutionary trajectory the tumor had evolved through. To investigate this, we teamed up with Mike Lawrence at MGH to examine the genomes of clones that had evolved resistance through the persister trajectory and compared them to clones that have evolved from preexisting T790M cells that effectively had never been suppressed by drug. We observed that cells evolving through the persister state harbored a much higher tumor mutation burden, and specifically an increase in APOBEC signature mutations. APOBEC enzymes are a family of cytidine deaminases that catalyze the deamination of cytosine to uracil in single-stranded DNA and RNA. APOBECs have come to be known as a major source of mutagenesis in cancers, giving rise to C to T and C to G mutations in TPC motifs comprising Sanger signatures SBS2 and 13. APOBEC mutational signatures are enriched in virally mediated cancers such as HPV-associated head and neck cancer and cervical cancer, but they also occur in non-virally mediated cancers such as subsets of lung cancer and breast cancer. We surveyed the 11 APOBEC family members in a panel of EGFR and ALK lung cancer cell lines treated with targeted therapies. We observed heterogeneous expression levels of the different APOBECs, but consistently, we saw that APOBEC3A was upregulated after drug treatment and stayed elevated in persister cells. To understand whether this led to increased mutagenesis in these cells, we took advantage of the fact that APOBEC3A preferentially edits TPC motifs within structurally conserved hairpin loops. In particular, an mRNA hotspot in the DDOSD transcript provides a reliable way of assessing APOBEC editing in the lab. We observed, consistent with the increase in APOBEC3 expression, that RNA editing at this DDOSC hotspot increased in EGFR and ALK mutant lung cancer cell lines treated with targeted therapy.We subsequently have corroborated these findings by developing a live cell editing reporter that can read out APOBEC editing in living intact cells. In this reporter, we engineered APOBEC substrate hairpin into a hiBIT sequence, which disrupts complementation between hiBIT and largeBIT. Upon APOBEC3A editing, the hiBIT is converted into a form that can complement with largeBIT, generating a luminescent signal. Using this reporter, we observed that drug-induced APOBEC editing occurred within one to two days after treatment, and we were also able to quantify APOBEC in living cells representing different targeted therapy contexts and different APOBEC editing levels. So what are the consequences of APOBEC editing in persister cells? Persister cells bear hallmarks of DNA damage, shown here by increased gamma H2AX staining. In addition, persister cells exhibit increased double-strand DNA breaks, shown here by comet assay. When we knock down APOBEC3A in these cells, we can completely reverse the increase in double-strand DNA breaks. If we knock down APOBEC3B, we can also partially reverse this phenotype. As expected, we noted increased APOBEC mutations and structural variations in the control cells.
