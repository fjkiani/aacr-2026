Topoisomerase 1 inhibitors trap topoisomerase 1 during DNA cleavage, generating topoisomerase DNA cleavage complexes, also referred to as TOP1 cleavage complexes. The collision of replication forks and transcription complexes with TOP1 cleavage complexes generate irreversible cleavage complexes as cleaved DNA templates become misaligned. Subsequently, there's the generation of double-stranded DNA breaks. The repair of these cleavage complexes relies in part on PARP, which promotes replication fork reversal to prevent collisions, and via PARylation recruits TDP1 to excise these cleavage complexes. Therefore, PARP inhibition potentiates the DNA-damaging effects of TOP1 inhibition. Of note, synergy between PARP inhibitors and TOP1 inhibitors occurs due to PARP catalytic inhibition and is independent of PARP trapping ability. Multiple lines of pre-clinical evidence supports PARP inhibitor potentiation of TOP1 inhibitor-induced cytotoxicity. In clinical studies, however, substantial rates of hematologic toxicity, even when using a weak PARP trapper such as veliparib, offset by low rates of clinical activity, have limited combinations of PARP inhibitors with a classical TOP1 inhibiting chemotherapy. Therefore, we hypothesized that targeted delivery of a TOP1 inhibitor via an antibody drug conjugate may reduce systemic toxicity and improve anti-tumor efficacy. We sought to evaluate this using DS-8201A, more commonly known as trastuzumab deruxtecan or T-DXd, which is a HER2-targeting ADC carrying a potent exatecan derivative TOP1 inhibiting payload with a drug antibody ratio of approximately eight. At the time of trial initiation, DS-8201A was only indicated for use in select breast and gastroesophageal cancers. We sought to combine DS-8201A with olaparib, a PARP inhibitor which was indicated only for select ovarian, breast, pancreatic, and prostate cancers at the time of study start. We sequentially explored three dosing schedules of olaparib with DS-8201A in three modules in dose escalation. This illustration depicts the rationale for gapped schedule olaparib dosing studied in two of the dose escalation modules. The rationale here is that after administration of a TOP1 inhibitor, TOP1 cleavage complexes remain loaded in tumor cells, as depicted in the green areas under the curve, while they are cleared from normal tissues, as depicted in the red areas under the curve. Therefore, administration of a PARP inhibitor a few days after a TOP1 inhibitor may maximize tumor DNA damage while mitigating effects in normal tissues. However, as the optimal timing of PARP inhibition after TOP1 inhibition is not yet known, we explored differing schedules in modules two and three. In this Phase 1 study, we began enrollment in module one, evaluating continuously dosed olaparib given days one through 21 of each 21-day cycle of treatment. We subsequently explored gap schedule olaparib in module two, where it was dosed days eight through 14, and in module three, where olaparib was given days three through nine. We enrolled adult patients with HER2-positive advanced solid tumors, defined as those with HER2 IHC expression of one plus or greater, or ERBB2 amplification.
