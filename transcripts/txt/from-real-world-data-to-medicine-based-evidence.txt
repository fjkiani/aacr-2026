So we have heard seven presentations that speak to the breadth of what real world data can deliver. Real world data is health information collected outside clinical trials from electronic health records, registries, claims databases, and genomic platforms.Real-world evidence is what we generate when we analyze that data with scientific rigor. Both have the potential to move the bar clinically. For example, the FDA has formally recognized that real-world evidence can complement, or even in select cases, substitute for traditional trial evidence, as codified in the 21st Century Cures Act of 2016, which was designed to accelerate medical product development and bring new innovations and advances faster and more efficiently to the patients who need them. Real-world evidence has already contributed to oncology drug approvals and label expansions. In the device space, more than 100 medical device submissions between 2020 and 2024 incorporated real-world evidence, with over half of them supporting regulatory approval. On the safety side, the Sentinel Initiative and post-marketing surveillance programs have used real-world data to inform labeling updates. So when we consider that roughly 40% of cancer patients would be ineligible for the trials that define their treatment, and that very few patients have access and resources to participate in clinical trials, reliance on real-world data is not optional, it is essential. The seven studies in this session show us how real-world data can range from hypothesis generating to confirmation of our clinical intuitions. So here are our key takeaways. Dr. Longshore and colleagues conducted a large real-world retrospective analysis of molecular testing data over 10 years from a commercial diagnostics laboratory to characterize variant level frequencies of actionable biomarkers in non-small cell lung cancer. Dr. Tran and colleagues characterized the lung cancer in never smokers' histologic landscape, and for non-mucinous adenocarcinoma, evaluated the prognostic value of the International Association for the Study of Lung Cancer grade system and additional histologic features in large data sets. Dr. Shaw and colleagues performed a large real-world outcome analysis of HER2-amplified microsatellite stable metastatic colorectal cancer, comparing TDxd with standard chemotherapy. Dr. Himbert and colleagues conducted a cohort analysis using the Veterans Health Administration data to compare cancer outcomes among patients who received ARBs versus ACE inhibitors. Dr. Lin and colleagues leveraged national data from Taiwan to identify associations between reproductive technology and the risk of breast, ovarian, and endometrial cancers. Dr. Suth and colleagues shared innovative work being done to address the global imbalance in genomic evidence generation through a real-world clinical genomics platform, integrating prospective clinico-genomic data from India with validated precision oncology infrastructure from Germany. Dr. Sahay and colleagues used the MSK Impact clinical cohort to train classifiers for detecting alternative lengthening of telomeres, a telomere maintenance mechanism observed in several aggressive cancer types. So here we see several unifying themes. First, scale unlocks discovery. 350,000 liquid biopsies, nearly 80,000 tumors analyzed for alternative lengthening of telomeres, 2.6 million veterans. These sample sizes facilitate the statistical power needed to detect buried signals. Second, extracting new knowledge from existing data. For example, repurposing discarded off-target sequencing reads, using EMR-based databases, validating a grading system in never smokers using archival slides. Indeed, the next breakthrough may already be in our data sets, waiting for the right question. Third, equity as a scientific imperative. For example, using liquid biopsies to democratize access to molecular profiling across variable settings, developing a platform generating multi-omics data for patients in India. These studies exemplify how real-world data supports precision oncology, as we cannot be precise if we are simply extrapolating data based on a narrow population in well-resourced settings. And fourth, but most important, bridging biology and the bedside. Each study generated findings with direct clinical relevance. How do we assess tumor mutation status in community-based settings? Are there more subtle implications in antihypertensive selection? Should cancer screening be informed by fertility treatment?
