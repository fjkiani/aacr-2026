So high-grade serous ovarian carcinoma, or HSOC, is the most lethal subtype of ovarian cancer with a five-year survival rate of 41%. This survival rate drops to less than 15% over 10 years. Unlike many other cancers, it's not driven by recurrent point mutations, but by widespread copy number alterations across the genome. This genomic instability shapes key features of the disease, including the tumor microenvironment, treatment response, and ultimately survival outcomes. So understanding patterns of copy number alteration is critical to understanding both tumor biology and clinical outcomes in HSOC. Over the past several decades, we've seen meaningful improvements in five-year survival for ovarian cancer, but those gains have not been shared equally. As you can see here, five-year survival has steadily increased among White women, reaching around 50% in recent years. In contrast, survival among Black women has remained relatively flat, staying in the low 40% range. So despite overall progress, these gains have not been shared equally, highlighting a persistent and widening disparity where one group benefits from advances in treatment and care, and another is largely left behind. This reinforces the need to better understand both biological and systemic factors contributing to these inequities. Building on these disparities, an important question is what's driving them? While differences in access to care and treatment certainly play a role, outcome disparities persist even after accounting for these factors, even among patients receiving guideline-concordant care, suggesting that this is not the full explanation. At the same time, our understanding of tumor biology and HSOC is based largely on cohorts of predominantly European ancestry, where race and ethnicity are often under-reported. This limits both generalizability and our ability to detect population-specific genomic patterns. Importantly, self-reported race is not a biological variable. It reflects social and structural exposures that shape health outcomes. So if we want to understand whether tumor biology differs, we need more precise ways to capture genetic ancestry and integrate that into our analyses.
