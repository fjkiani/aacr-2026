The 2026 AACR Award for Lifetime Achievement in Cancer Research is being awarded to Dr. James P. Allison of the University of Texas MD Anderson Cancer Center. Dr. Allison's discovery and development of immune checkpoint blockade therapy revolutionized cancer immunotherapy and reshaped the clinical landscape across many cancer types. Early in his career, Dr. Allison focused on understanding the fundamental biology of T cell activation and regulation. His laboratory identified key components of the T cell response, including the T cell receptor, which recognizes antigens presented by MHC molecules, and CD28, a critical co-stimulatory receptor required for full T cell activation. In the mid-1990s, Dr. Allison made a landmark discovery by demonstrating that CTLA-4 functions as a negative regulator of T cell activation, a brake on the immune response. At a time when most efforts in immunotherapy focused on stimulating the immune system, Dr. Allison proposed a bold and unconventional idea that blocking inhibitory pathways could unleash antitumor immunity. His laboratory tested this hypothesis in preclinical models, showing that inhibition of CTLA-4 led to complete tumor rejection. This work laid the foundation for a new therapeutic paradigm, immune checkpoint blockade, a term coined by Dr. Allison. Translating this concept into the clinic required extraordinary persistence and vision, particularly in the face of skepticism and prior failures in immunotherapy. Ultimately, this work led to the development of the anti-CTLA-4 antibody ipilimumab, which in 2011 became the first immune checkpoint inhibitor approved by the FDA after demonstrating a survival benefit in patients with metastatic melanoma. Notably, a subset of these patients experienced long-term, durable remissions lasting more than a decade. Dr. Allison's discovery catalyzed the development of an entirely new class of cancer therapies, including inhibitors targeting PD-1, PD-L1 pathway. Today, immune checkpoint blockade therapies are approved across numerous cancer types and have provided lasting benefit with improved survival to hundreds of thousands of patients worldwide. Importantly, Dr. Allison's work established a new principle in oncology that targeting the immune system, rather than the tumor alone, can produce durable and potentially curative responses. This paradigm shift continues to guide the development of next-generation immunotherapies and combination strategies. Dr. Allison remains at the forefront of cancer research, leading efforts to understand mechanisms of response and resistance to immunotherapy and to expand its benefits to more patients. He currently leads the James P. Allison Institute at the University of Texas MD Anderson Cancer Center. Among his many honorees, Dr. Allison was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 2018 for his groundbreaking discoveries in cancer immunotherapy.
