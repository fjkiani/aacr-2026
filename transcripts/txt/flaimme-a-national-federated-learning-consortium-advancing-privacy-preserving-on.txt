Another issue is that models trained at a single institution often don't perform well elsewhere because patient populations and clinical practices can differ significantly across institutions. So federated learning offers a potential way forward. Instead of moving data to a central location, it allows us to bring the model to the data for training, enabling collaboration across institutions while keeping sensitive information secure and local. And in doing so, this opens the door to learning from broader, more diverse data sets and creating AI models that are more robust and generalizable. So let me walk you through how federated learning works at a high level. Federated learning starts with initializing a global model on a central server, and this model is the basis from which the federated learning process begins. This model is provided to each of the sites in the network. Now, each institution keeps its data locally and trains that model on its own data. The data could include clinical records, imaging, genomic data, depending on the task. After a round of training at each site, instead of sharing raw data, each site sends only the model updates, essentially what the model has learned, to a central coordinating server. And this server aggregates these updates to create an improved global model, which is then sent back to all the participating institutions. And this process can be repeated multiple times, allowing the model to learn from a much broader and more diverse data set, again, without the data ever having to leave its original location. And so the federated learning enables collaboration across institutions, ideally improving model robustness and helps protect patient privacy. So to explore the feasibility of federated learning across cancer centers, the NCI initiated the FLAME Consortium in 2003. The original goals of the FLAME Consortium were really proof of concept. Specifically, testing whether sites could exchange a model and federate using a common technical infrastructure and governance approach. The founding FLAME sites were the NCI, CHOP, and OHSU in 2003.
